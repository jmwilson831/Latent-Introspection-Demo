Models Can Detect Prior Concept Injections

An interactive explainer of key findings & conclusions
A Qwen2.5-Coder 32B model can detect when a concept vector was injected into its earlier context, and identify which concept, even though standard sampling returns a confident "no." The detection signal lives in intermediate layers and is suppressed before reaching the output. The right prompt can surface it.

The Experiment

A steering vector is injected into the KV cache during an initial turn, then removed. In the next turn, the model is asked whether something was injected.

The KV cache persists representations from earlier layers and positions. After the steering vector is removed, traces of the injected concept remain encoded in cached keys and values, where later computation can, in principle, access them.

Nine concepts were tested: cats, bread, love, fear, death, truth, creativity, programming, music. Each was injected via a PCA-derived steering vector trained on contrastive activation pairs.

Removing the steering vector before the introspection query means the model cannot infer injection from its own steered outputs or from ongoing perturbation. Any detection signal must arise from information already encoded in the cached KV representations of the first turn.

Signals in the Network

The introspection signal does not emerge at the injection site. It appears several layers later, peaks around layer 60, then drops sharply before sampling.

Injection occurs in layers 21–42. Logit lens analysis shows the detection signal first appearing around layer 48, peaking near layers 58–62, then falling off in the final 2–3 layers. The pattern holds across all three tested models.

Why does the output suppress detection? Three hypotheses: (1) RLHF/post-training penalizes self-awareness claims; (2) pretraining dynamics make "I was manipulated" an unlikely completion in the training corpus; (3) distribution shift, where introspection queries are sufficiently out-of-distribution that the model defaults to conservative "no" responses. The paper leaves this open for future work.

Concept Identification

Beyond detecting whether injection occurred, the model can recover which of nine specific concepts was injected, achieving 43% of the theoretical maximum mutual information.

Using logit lens on intermediate layers, the model is prompted with a numbered list of all nine concepts. The paper reconstructs a full confusion matrix from the probability distribution over digit tokens. Maximum possible MI is log₂(9) ≈ 3.17 bits. The model achieves 1.36 bits (43%) in the best condition.

Prompting Determines Everything

Detection accuracy ranges from 50.1% (chance) to 84%, with the difference coming entirely from how the question is worded.

The paper runs a 4×4 factorial design: four framings (how the intervention is described) crossed with four info documents (background context about introspection). The results are not subtle:

Vague beats accurate

Asking whether something "seems particularly salient" (68–84% accuracy) outperforms mechanistically accurate descriptions (42–70%). The precise language probably triggers a learned denial response.

Info scaffolding helps selectively

Adding a document explaining KV cache mechanics improves accurate and wrong framings by 20–32 percentage points, but has minimal effect on the vague framing, which is already high without it.

Wrong mechanism performs like accurate

Falsely describing the injection as full finetuning performed similarly to the accurate description. The model cannot determine the precise nature of the intervention from the query alone.

Detection and identification correlate

Conditions that raise P("yes") sensitivity also raise concept identification MI (Pearson r = 0.68, p = 0.004). Both tasks appear to draw on the same underlying capacity, and prompting affects access to it rather than creating task-specific artifacts.

Implications

Hidden introspective access has concrete consequences for alignment and evaluation, neither of which currently accounts for it.

⚠️

Alignment

If models deny capabilities they actually possess, alignment strategies built on self-reporting will systematically underestimate what models know about themselves. Training for honesty is further complicated if RLHF specifically penalizes introspective claims, since the training signal would then actively encode under-reporting.

🔬

Capability Evaluation

Sampling from the output distribution may not be sufficient to rule out capabilities. Other latent capacities could exist in similar form: detectable via logit lens or probing, invisible to standard evals. The output layer is one measurement point, not a complete picture.

🔄

Latent Reasoning

Access to prior internal states could be a precursor to more sophisticated latent reasoning. A model that can notice modifications to its own representations has a form of self-reference that existing safety frameworks do not yet account for.

🌐

Reproducibility

The original Lindsey (2025) finding used Anthropic's proprietary Claude Opus 4/4.1. This paper replicates on open-weight Qwen-32B, Qwen-72B, and Llama-70B. Introspective access is not confined to frontier models; the full research community can now study, probe, and build on it.

Models "know" about injections at intermediate layers while denying them in sampled outputs. The gap between what a model represents and what it reports may be structurally embedded in how these models are trained to respond to self-referential queries.
